# Open Questions

Unresolved questions, areas for further research, and potential weaknesses in the exchangification thesis.

---

## On the Thesis Itself

### Is this actually novel?
The individual observations (AI reduces transaction costs, marketplaces evolve, commoditization is accelerating) are well-established. Is "exchangification" a genuinely new synthesis, or just a rebrand of existing ideas? The Coasean Singularity paper (NBER, 2025) covers adjacent territory. What does this thesis add beyond what economists already understand?

**What would settle it**: Survey existing literature more exhaustively. If no one has drawn the specific connection between AI standardization → exchange formation across non-financial categories, the thesis is novel. If they have, the thesis needs a differentiated angle.

### Is the marketplace → exchange transition inevitable, or contingent?
The historical pattern (bazaar → marketplace → exchange) may not be a natural law. It could be a pattern that applied to specific categories under specific conditions. The conditions for exchangification may include: (1) the units being traded are actually standardizable, (2) sufficient volume, (3) regulatory permission, (4) both sides of the market prefer exchange dynamics to relationship dynamics. Not all of these hold for all categories.

**What would settle it**: Identify categories that *could* have been exchangified but weren't — and understand why. Failed exchanges are as informative as successful ones.

### Does AI actually improve standardization, or does it just create the illusion of standardization?
An AI grading system that assigns a quality score to a freelancer doesn't actually make freelancers fungible — it just creates a number that *pretends* they are. If the underlying variance is real and meaningful (which it often is), AI grading may lead to worse outcomes than marketplace-style trust mechanisms (reviews, relationships, track records).

**What would settle it**: Empirical evidence from AI grading systems. Do AI quality scores predict actual outcomes better than reputation scores? In which categories? Under what conditions?

## On the Economics

### Who captures the value?
In historical exchangifications, the exchange operator captured enormous value (NYSE, CME, Google Ad Exchange). But this was often through monopoly or near-monopoly positions. Will AI-era exchanges be natural monopolies, or will low barriers to entry create fragmented, competitive exchange ecosystems?

**What would settle it**: Study the competitive dynamics of existing AI-era "exchanges" (GPU spot markets, freight exchanges, programmatic ad platforms). Are they consolidating or fragmenting?

### What happens to the "middle"?
The thesis predicts bifurcation: exchange tier (commodity) and artisan tier (premium). But most economic activity happens in the middle. What happens to "good enough" providers in a bifurcated world? Mass unemployment? Forced up-skilling? Race to the bottom?

**What would settle it**: Look at categories where exchangification already happened (advertising, equities) and study what happened to mid-tier participants. Did they move up, move down, or exit?

### Does exchangification increase or decrease total market size?
Historical precedent suggests exchangification dramatically increases transaction volume (more liquidity = more trades). But it may also reduce total spending if prices compress. Does the volume increase outweigh the price decrease?

**What would settle it**: Market size data before and after exchangification in historical categories.

## On AI Specifically

### Will AI agents actually be exchange participants?
The thesis assumes AI agents will autonomously transact, creating the high-frequency activity that exchanges require. But agent-to-agent (A2A) commerce is still nascent. Will consumers actually trust AI to buy on their behalf? Will regulations permit it?

**What would settle it**: Track adoption of AI purchasing agents over the next 2-3 years. Bain (2026) says 30-45% of consumers already use GenAI for product research. When does "research" become "purchase"?

### Does AI create new frictions that offset the transaction cost reduction?
The Coasean Singularity paper identifies new AI-specific frictions: congestion (millions of agents hitting the same endpoints), price obfuscation (agents gaming each other), algorithmic collusion, and quality degradation from automated shortcuts. Could these new frictions prevent exchangification?

**What would settle it**: Study early A2A commerce environments for emergent friction patterns.

### What about the "digital feudalism" counterargument?
The CMR Berkeley piece argues AI agent proliferation could create platform gatekeepers more powerful than current marketplaces — "digital feudalism" rather than open exchanges. If AI agents operate within walled gardens, we get platform monopolies, not open exchanges.

**What would settle it**: Watch whether AI agent protocols (MCP, etc.) remain open or get captured by large platforms.

## On Specific Categories

### Labor: Can human work actually be made fungible?
This is the most consequential and most uncertain category. AI can grade coding ability, writing quality, and customer service performance. But does that grading capture what matters? Is a "90th percentile" AI-graded developer actually interchangeable with another "90th percentile" developer? Human work has dimensions (communication, creativity, cultural fit, growth potential) that resist quantification.

### Real estate: Does tokenization solve the fungibility problem?
Tokenizing property creates fractional, tradeable units — but the underlying asset is still unique. A token representing 1/1000th of a Manhattan apartment is not the same as a token representing 1/1000th of a suburban house. REITs already partially solved this (portfolios of properties create fungibility at the fund level). Does tokenization add anything REITs don't already provide?

### Healthcare: Will patients accept exchange dynamics?
Even if healthcare services could be standardized and exchange-traded, will patients accept it? Healthcare decisions are emotional, high-stakes, and deeply personal. The market might resist exchangification even if the economics support it.

### Carbon: Is the measurement problem solvable?
Carbon credit exchangification depends on reliable measurement and verification. If AI-powered MRV can't definitively answer "did this project actually reduce emissions by X tons?", the grading system has no integrity, and the exchange collapses (as it nearly did in 2023 when major verification scandals hit the voluntary market).

## On New Frictions (from Coasean Singularity research)

### Do AI agents create new frictions that replace old ones?
The NBER paper finds that while AI agents reduce search, communication, and contracting costs, they introduce *new* frictions: congestion (millions of agents hitting the same endpoints), price obfuscation (agents gaming each other), and algorithmic collusion. Critics sharpen this further: when the marginal cost of running an agent approaches zero, the scarce resource shifts to where the agent can run and what it can access. The firm that hosts the compute, owns the data, or controls the model becomes the effective landlord of the agent economy. Old frictions don't vanish — they mutate into **access rents**.

**What would settle it**: Map the friction landscape of early agent-to-agent commerce environments. Are new frictions growing faster than old frictions are shrinking?

### Is the asymptotic limit the key insight?
The most likely outcome per the research: transaction costs drop dramatically but never quite reach zero due to persistent frictions (regulation, trust, coordination). This actually *strengthens* the bifurcation thesis — the artisan tier survives precisely because the last mile of cost reduction is the hardest. The interesting question is: how far does the asymptote go in each category? The categories where costs get closest to zero are the ones that exchangify most completely.

**What would settle it**: Quantify the "residual friction floor" in already-exchangified categories (advertising, equities). How low did costs actually go?

### The $3-5T agent commerce prediction
McKinsey projects $3-5T in annual retail commerce orchestrated by AI agents by 2030. Gartner predicts 90% of B2B purchases handled by AI agents by 2028. If even half true, this creates the transaction volume needed for exchange dynamics in categories that currently lack liquidity.

**What would settle it**: Track actual agent commerce volume quarterly starting 2026.

## On Newly Identified Categories

### AI inference tokens: The fastest exchangification in history?
Token spot markets already exceed $10B. A March 2026 paper proposes standardized token futures. If successful, this would be a category that went from *nonexistent* to *exchange-traded commodity* in under 5 years. But tokens aren't truly fungible — a Claude token isn't an GPT token isn't a Gemini token. Will model differentiation prevent true exchange dynamics, or will routing/aggregation layers (OpenRouter, Martian) create effective fungibility?

### Can consulting survive AI commoditization of knowledge?
If baseline expertise becomes free (AI can produce a market sizing, competitive analysis, or process map), what's left? Implementation, relationships, and political cover. But those are the things that *can't* be exchangified. Does consulting bifurcate into exchange-traded deliverables + artisan advisory? Or does the whole industry collapse because the commodity tier was subsidizing the premium tier?

### Pharma R&D: Are molecules the new grain?
Standardized molecular representations (SMILES, molecular graphs) + AI generative chemistry = molecules as tradeable units. Royalty Pharma already trades royalty streams as financial instruments. But drug development has a 90%+ failure rate — can you exchange-trade something with that much uncertainty? Or does the failure rate *itself* become the basis for financial instruments (like credit default swaps)?

## Research Priorities

1. **Empirical grading accuracy**: Find data on AI grading/quality assessment accuracy in non-financial categories (legal, coding, real estate, insurance). This is the linchpin.
2. **Failed exchanges**: Study categories where exchange formation was attempted and failed. What were the conditions?
3. **Agent commerce adoption**: Track real data on AI agent purchasing behavior and consumer trust. McKinsey's $3-5T prediction is the benchmark.
4. **Regulatory landscape**: Map the regulatory barriers to exchangification in key categories (healthcare, legal, labor, real estate).
5. **Incumbent response**: How are current marketplace companies (Upwork, Airbnb, Zillow) thinking about exchange dynamics?
6. **Token futures feasibility**: Track progress on the arxiv token futures proposal. Who will operate the first AI compute exchange?
7. **New friction mapping**: Document the emerging frictions in agent-to-agent commerce (congestion, price obfuscation, access rents). Are they growing faster than old frictions shrink?
8. **Residual friction floor**: Quantify how low transaction costs actually went in already-exchangified categories. This tells us the realistic limit for new categories.
9. **B2B procurement agent adoption**: Track Gartner's 90% prediction. This is the highest-volume, highest-dollar exchangification currently in motion.
