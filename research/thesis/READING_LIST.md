# Reading List: The Exchangification Thesis

An annotated bibliography organized by theme. Each entry includes the key insight relevant to the exchangification thesis.

---

## Foundational Economics

### Transaction Cost Theory

**Ronald Coase — "The Nature of the Firm" (1937)**
The foundational paper. Firms exist because market transaction costs (search, bargaining, enforcement) make it cheaper to organize production internally. *Exchangification relevance*: AI reduces every category of transaction cost, shifting the efficient boundary from firm → market → exchange.

**Oliver Williamson — "The Economic Institutions of Capitalism" (1985)**
Extended Coase with the concepts of asset specificity, bounded rationality, and opportunism as drivers of organizational form. *Exchangification relevance*: AI reduces bounded rationality (better information processing) and polices opportunism (continuous monitoring), making markets/exchanges viable where firms were previously necessary.

**Shahidi, Rusak, Manning, Fradkin, Horton — "The Coasean Singularity? Demand, Supply, and Market Design with AI Agents" (NBER Working Paper 34468, 2025)**
[NBER](https://www.nber.org/papers/w34468)
The most directly relevant academic paper. Argues AI agents will transform digital markets by reducing transaction costs toward zero, enabling "personalized bundling, micro-contracts, and zero-trust marketplaces." Identifies competing forces: efficiency gains vs. new frictions (congestion, price obfuscation, algorithmic collusion).

**California Management Review — "From Coase to AI Agents: Why the Economics of the Firm Still Matters in the Age of Automation" (2025)**
[CMR Berkeley](https://cmr.berkeley.edu/2025/04/from-coase-to-ai-agents-why-the-economics-of-the-firm-still-matters-in-the-age-of-automation/)
Warns that uncontrolled AI agent proliferation could create "digital feudalism" where platform providers gain gatekeeping power. Counterargument to pure exchangification optimism.

**Tyler Cowen / Marginal Revolution — "Will there be a Coasean singularity?" (2025)**
[Marginal Revolution](https://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2025/10/will-there-be-a-coasean-singularity.html)
Cowen's reaction to the NBER paper. Important for the skeptical/nuanced perspective.

### Market Design

**Alvin Roth — "Who Gets What — and Why: The New Economics of Matchmaking and Market Design" (2015)**
Nobel laureate's work on matching markets — markets where "prices don't do all the work." Designed kidney exchanges, medical residency matching, school choice systems. *Exchangification relevance*: Roth's work shows that market design (the rules and mechanisms) matters as much as the assets being traded. AI changes what market designs are feasible.

**Paul Milgrom & Robert Wilson — Auction Theory (Nobel Prize 2020)**
Foundational work on auction design and price discovery mechanisms. The FCC spectrum auctions they designed are a direct example of exchangification — turning radio spectrum from a negotiated government allocation into an exchange-traded asset. *Exchangification relevance*: Auction theory provides the mechanism design toolkit for building exchanges.

### Market Microstructure

**Maureen O'Hara — "Market Microstructure Theory" (1995)**
Academic treatment of how markets actually work at the transaction level — price discovery, information asymmetry, market maker behavior. *Exchangification relevance*: Provides the theoretical framework for understanding when and why exchanges emerge.

**Harvard Business School — "The Origin and Development of Markets"**
[HBS PDF](https://www.hbs.edu/businesshistory/Documents/origin-and-development-of-markets.pdf)
Historical survey of how formal markets evolved from informal exchange. Physical infrastructure (market halls, standardized measures) preceded institutional infrastructure (rules, clearing).

**Jean-Charles Rochet & Jean Tirole — "Platform Competition in Two-Sided Markets" (2003)**
*Journal of the European Economic Association*. Nobel-winning framework for two-sided market economics — pricing structure, network effects, and competition. *Exchangification relevance*: Exchanges are two-sided markets with maximum standardization. Understanding when platforms tip toward exchange dynamics requires this framework.

**Yakov Amihud & Haim Mendelson — "Asset Pricing and the Bid-Ask Spread" (1986)**
Foundational work on how liquidity (tight spreads, transparent pricing) attracts participants and improves asset prices. *Exchangification relevance*: Explains the self-reinforcing dynamics of exchanges — liquidity begets liquidity — and why the marketplace→exchange transition tends to be one-directional.

## Platform and Marketplace Theory

### Foundational

**Parker, Van Alstyne, Choudary — "Platform Revolution" (2016)**
Comprehensive treatment of platform economics: network effects, multi-sided markets, platform governance. *Exchangification relevance*: Exchanges are the most extreme form of platform — maximum standardization, maximum liquidity, minimum differentiation.

**David Evans & Richard Schmalensee — "Matchmakers: The New Economics of Multisided Platforms" (2016)**
How platforms create value by reducing search and transaction costs between two (or more) sides of a market. *Exchangification relevance*: Exchanges are matchmakers with zero tolerance for bespoke transactions.

### VC / Practitioner Perspectives

**Bill Gurley (Benchmark Capital) — Marketplace Writings**
[Gurley's Above the Crowd blog](http://abovethecrowd.com)
Gurley's key insight: "Highly liquid marketplaces naturally 'tip' towards becoming a clearinghouse where neither the consumer nor the supplier would favor an alternative." This IS the exchangification thesis stated from a VC perspective. His investments (Uber, OpenTable, Zillow) are all marketplace-to-exchange bets.

**a16z — "Marketplaces in the Age of AI, Take Two: Graveyard to Greenfield" (2025)**
[a16z](https://a16z.com/marketplaces-in-the-age-of-ai-take-two-graveyard-to-greenfield/)
Argues AI is reviving marketplace categories that previously failed (not creating new ones). AI fixes unit economics by reducing CAC and increasing LTV. Two mechanisms: "AI as middleman" (automating operations) and "changed value proposition" (transparency, fixed pricing). *Exchangification relevance*: The "graveyard to greenfield" framing is about categories entering the marketplace phase — the precursor to exchangification.

**a16z — "Marketplaces in the Age of AI" (2024)**
[a16z](https://a16z.com/marketplaces-in-the-age-of-ai/)
Earlier piece predicting AI-native marketplaces with "just-in-time product generation, agent-orchestrated price negotiation, and extremely personalized search." *Exchangification relevance*: Agent-orchestrated price negotiation IS exchange dynamics.

**NFX — "The Next 10 Years Will Be About the AI Agent Economy" (2025)**
[NFX](https://www.nfx.com/post/ai-agent-marketplaces)
Argues AI agents will organize into marketplaces, particularly for SMBs. Key insight: incumbent marketplaces won't build AI agent models that cannibalize their human providers. New entrants will. *Exchangification relevance*: AI agent marketplaces are the bridge between current marketplaces and future exchanges.

**NFX — "The AI-First Marketplace" (2024)**
[NFX](https://www.nfx.com/post/ai-first-marketplace)
Framework for how AI transforms marketplace structure: supply unlocking, demand-side embedding, search reimagination, internal efficiency. *Exchangification relevance*: Each of these four changes moves marketplaces closer to exchange dynamics.

**ARK Invest — "Are Marketplaces Defensible In The Age Of AI Purchasing Agents?" (2025)**
[ARK Invest](https://www.ark-invest.com/articles/analyst-research/are-marketplaces-defensible-in-the-age-of-ai-purchasing-agents)
Argues AI purchasing agents could commoditize marketplaces into "interchangeable, dumb pipes." If agents simply seek the best product, brand loyalty diminishes and marketplaces become commodity infrastructure. *Exchangification relevance*: This is the exchange thesis from the marketplace defender's nightmare perspective.

**Bain & Company — "Agentic AI Commerce: The Next Retail Revolution Is Here" (2026)**
[Bain](https://www.bain.com/insights/agentic-ai-commerce-the-next-retail-revolution-is-here/)
Predicts "full agent-to-agent (A2A) commerce" that could "bypass classic retail websites altogether." 30-45% of US consumers already use GenAI to research products. Retailers must choose: embrace third-party agents, build proprietary agents, or fortify owned channels. *Exchangification relevance*: A2A commerce IS exchange dynamics. Agents transacting directly, without human browsing, is the definition of an exchange.

## Historical Case Studies

### Financial Markets

**"Flash Boys" — Michael Lewis (2014)**
The story of HFT and how electronic trading transformed equity markets. *Exchangification relevance*: Shows the end-state of exchangification — when trading becomes so fast and algorithmic that human participation is no longer competitive.

**"The Man Who Solved the Market" — Gregory Zuckerman (2019)**
Jim Simons and Renaissance Technologies. *Exchangification relevance*: Demonstrates that once a market is exchangified, algorithmic participants extract value from less sophisticated ones.

### Commodity Markets

**CME Group — "Midwest Grain Trade: History of Futures Exchanges"**
[CME Group](https://www.cmegroup.com/education/courses/introduction-to-futures/midwest-grain-trade-history-of-futures-exchanges)
Primary source on how grain trading evolved from sample-based to standardized futures.

**Craig Pirrong — "The Successes and Failures of Commodity Exchanges"**
[Bauer UH PDF](https://www.bauer.uh.edu/spirrong/pirrong_commodity_exchanges_jls.pdf)
Academic analysis of why some commodity exchanges succeed and others fail. *Exchangification relevance*: Identifies the conditions necessary for exchange formation — lessons applicable to new categories.

### Advertising

**AdButler — "Ad Networks vs Ad Exchanges: The History of Programmatic Advertising"**
[AdButler](https://www.adbutler.com/blog/article/ad-networks-vs-ad-exchanges-the-history-of-programmatic-advertising/)
Clear narrative of the ad network → ad exchange transition. The most complete example of exchangification in the digital era.

## AI and Market Structure

**"AI, Transaction Costs, and a Quiet Shift Toward Self-Employment" (Labor Market Matters, 2025)**
[Labor Market Matters](https://www.labormarketmatters.com/p/ai-transaction-costs-and-a-quiet)
Argues AI is exerting a "transaction-cost shock" that expands self-employment and contract-based work. *Exchangification relevance*: The shift from employment to contracts is a precursor to exchangification of labor — contracts are more standardizable than employment relationships.

**Muness Castle — "Reducing Transaction Costs with AI-Native Workflows" (2025)**
[Muness.com](https://muness.com/posts/transaction-costs/)
Practical treatment of how AI reduces specific transaction costs in knowledge work. *Exchangification relevance*: Concrete examples of the Coasean mechanism at work.

**FourWeekMBA — "The Coase Theorem of AI: Transaction Costs Determine Architecture"**
[FourWeekMBA](https://fourweekmba.com/the-coase-theorem-of-ai-transaction-costs-determine-architecture/)
Popular treatment of how Coase's framework applies to AI system architecture. *Exchangification relevance*: Accessible introduction to the economic theory underlying the thesis.

## Adjacent / Contrarian Reading

**Shoshana Zuboff — "The Age of Surveillance Capitalism" (2019)**
The dark side: when everything becomes exchange-tradeable, who profits from the data that enables pricing? *Exchangification relevance*: The grading/standardization that enables exchanges requires data — and data extraction has costs.

**Cory Doctorow — "Enshittification" essays (2023-2026)**
Platform decay theory. Platforms start by being good to users, then extract value from users for business customers, then extract from everyone. *Exchangification relevance*: Exchanges could follow the same pattern — or could be more resistant because of competitive transparency.

**Nassim Taleb — "Antifragile" (2012)**
Standardization creates fragility. Exchange-traded categories may be efficient but brittle. *Exchangification relevance*: The strongest counterargument — exchangification optimizes for efficiency at the cost of resilience.

**Clayton Christensen — "The Innovator's Dilemma" (1997)**
As products commoditize, competitive dynamics shift from product features to efficiency and price — exactly the conditions that favor exchange structures over relationship-driven marketplaces. *Exchangification relevance*: AI accelerates the commoditization cycle Christensen described, pushing more categories into the zone where exchanges become viable.

**Scott Patterson — "The Quants" (2010)**
History of quantitative trading and how mathematical precision transformed financial markets. *Exchangification relevance*: Shows the end-state when exchanges meet algorithmic participants — pattern recognition, speed, and scale dominate. This is the future for every exchangified category.
